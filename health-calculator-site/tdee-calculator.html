What is TDEE? A Complete Guide

TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is the total number of calories your body burns in a 24-hour period. It's the single most important number for anyone who wants to manage their weight — whether you want to lose fat, build muscle, or simply maintain your current body weight.

Think of your TDEE as your body's daily energy budget. Every calorie you eat either goes toward fuelling your body (matching your TDEE) or becomes a surplus (stored as fat) or a deficit (burned from stored fat). Getting this number right is the foundation of any successful diet plan.

The Simple Rule: Eat at TDEE → Maintain weight. Eat below TDEE → Lose weight. Eat above TDEE → Gain weight. That's literally all nutrition science boils down to.

How is TDEE Calculated? The Science Behind It

Your TDEE is calculated in two steps: first your BMR, then multiplied by an activity factor.

Step 1: Calculate BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)

BMR is the number of calories your body needs just to stay alive — breathing, pumping blood, maintaining body temperature — if you were to rest in bed all day. We use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which is the most accurate formula for most people:

Mifflin-St Jeor Equation
For Men:
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5

For Women:
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Step 2: Multiply by Activity Multiplier

Your BMR is then multiplied by an activity factor to account for how much energy you spend moving, exercising, and working:

Activity LevelMultiplierWho This Fits
Sedentary1.2×Office jobs, minimal walking, no exercise
Lightly Active1.375×Light exercise 1–3 days/week, some daily walking
Moderately Active1.55×Gym 3–5 days/week, active lifestyle
Very Active1.725×Hard exercise 6–7 days/week, physically demanding job
Super Active1.9×Athlete-level training, physically intense occupation

Most working Indians fall in the Sedentary to Lightly Active range — especially those in IT, education, or office-based roles. Don't overestimate your activity level; this is the most common mistake people make.

What Are Calories and Why Do They Matter?

A calorie is a unit of energy. In nutrition, when we say "1 calorie," we technically mean 1 kilocalorie (kcal) — the energy needed to raise 1 litre of water by 1°C. Every food you eat contains calories (energy), and your body uses those calories to power everything you do.

TDEE and Weight Loss: How to Use This Number

Once you know your TDEE, you have a precise target for your calorie intake. Here's how to use it:

1

Calculate your TDEE

Use the calculator above. This is your maintenance number — eating this many calories keeps your weight stable.

2

Choose your goal and set a deficit or surplus

For weight loss: subtract 300–500 calories from TDEE. For muscle gain: add 200–300 calories. For maintenance: eat at TDEE.

3

Track your food for 2–3 weeks

Use an app like HealthifyMe or Cronometer to log meals. Indian food databases are now very comprehensive.

4

Adjust based on real results

If you're not losing weight after 2 weeks, reduce by another 100–150 calories. TDEE is an estimate — your body may vary.

TDEE and Weight Gain / Muscle Building

If you want to build muscle, you need to eat above your TDEE — this is called a calorie surplus. However, a large surplus doesn't mean faster muscle gain; it just means more fat gain. Aim for a modest surplus of 200–350 kcal/day if you're a natural lifter.

For Indians following a vegetarian diet, muscle building requires deliberate attention to protein. Most Indians eating a standard diet consume only 30–50g of protein per day — far below the 1.6–2.2g/kg recommended for muscle growth. Your TDEE determines your total calories, but protein intake determines how much of your weight gain is actually muscle.

Why TDEE Varies Between Individuals

Two people of the same height, weight, and age can have meaningfully different TDEEs. The key factors:

Muscle Mass

Muscle burns 3× more calories at rest than fat tissue. A person with more muscle has a higher BMR and therefore a higher TDEE. This is why resistance training is the best long-term strategy — it raises your TDEE permanently.

Thyroid Function

Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) is very common in India, especially among women, and can reduce BMR by 15–20%. If you're eating well below your calculated TDEE and still not losing weight, a thyroid test is a good idea.

Age

BMR drops roughly 1–2% per decade after age 20, largely due to gradual muscle loss (sarcopenia). This is why staying active and eating enough protein becomes increasingly important as you age.

NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)

NEAT is the energy burned from all non-exercise movement — walking to the kitchen, fidgeting, cleaning, commuting. It can vary by 600–800 kcal/day between individuals and is a huge hidden factor in TDEE. People who are naturally fidgety or who walk a lot burn significantly more calories without "working out."

Frequently Asked Questions About TDEE

What is the difference between BMR and TDEE? +

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the calories your body burns at complete rest — just keeping your organs running. TDEE is BMR multiplied by your activity level. TDEE is always higher than BMR and is the more practical number for planning your diet.

How accurate is the TDEE calculator? +

The Mifflin-St Jeor formula used here has about ±10% accuracy for most people. It's an estimate, not an exact measure. Track your weight for 2–3 weeks while eating at your calculated TDEE, then adjust based on what actually happens. This empirical approach is more reliable than any formula alone.

Should I eat my TDEE calories on rest days? +

Your TDEE already accounts for your weekly average activity. You don't need to eat less on rest days unless you calculated using a very high activity multiplier. Overly cutting calories on rest days can actually impair muscle recovery. Many people eat roughly the same calories every day for simplicity.

Why is my actual weight loss slower than my calorie deficit predicts? +

Several reasons: water retention (especially early on), the body's metabolic adaptation to lower calorie intake, slight inaccuracies in food tracking, and the fact that 0.5 kg of body weight is not exactly 3,500 kcal of pure fat. Be patient — consistent 4-week trends matter more than weekly fluctuations.

Can I use TDEE for a vegetarian Indian diet? +

Absolutely. TDEE is calorie-based, not diet-specific. Whether you're vegetarian or non-vegetarian, the total calorie target stays the same. The key difference is that vegetarians need to be more intentional about protein — combining dal, paneer, curd, tofu, and legumes to hit protein goals.

How often should I recalculate my TDEE? +

Recalculate every 4–6 weeks during a weight loss phase, or whenever your weight changes by more than 3–4 kg. As you lose weight, your BMR decreases (you're a smaller body burning less), so your calorie target must adjust downward.

Is TDEE different for Indians compared to Western populations? +

The formula is the same, but on average Indians tend to have a higher body fat percentage at the same BMI compared to Caucasians. This means the activity multiplier may slightly overestimate actual TDEE for some Indians. Starting conservatively (e.g., one step lower on the activity scale) and adjusting empirically is a good approach.
