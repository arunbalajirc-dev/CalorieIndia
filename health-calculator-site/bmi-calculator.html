What is BMI?

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a simple numerical measure calculated from your height and weight. It was developed in the 1830s by Belgian statistician Adolphe Quetelet as a population-level screening tool — not an individual diagnostic measure. Despite its limitations, it remains one of the most widely used health indicators in the world because of its simplicity and correlation with health risk at a population level.

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height² (m²)

For example, a person who is 170 cm (1.70 m) tall and weighs 70 kg has a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.70 × 1.70) = 24.2.

BMI Chart for Indians: Asian vs. Western Standards

This is the most important thing to understand about BMI if you're Indian: standard Western BMI cutoffs are wrong for you.

Multiple studies, including landmark research from the Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR) and WHO's expert panel on Asian BMI, have shown that South Asians develop obesity-related health conditions like Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease at lower BMI values than Western populations. Why Do Indians Need Different BMI Ranges?

The "Thin-Fat Indian" Phenomenon

Indian doctors coined the term "thin-fat Indian" to describe a common pattern: South Asians who appear lean or have a "normal" Western BMI but carry a high percentage of body fat — particularly visceral fat around the organs. This visceral fat is more metabolically dangerous than subcutaneous fat (the fat under your skin).

Research Evidence

A study published in The Lancet analysed data from over 800,000 individuals across South, East and Southeast Asia. It found that the optimal BMI cutoff for identifying risk of Type 2 diabetes in South Asians is around 22–23, compared to 25 in European populations. The WHO also convened an expert consultation specifically on this issue and recommended action points (overweight) starting at BMI 23 for Asian populations.

Muscle Mass Differences

Indians on average have lower skeletal muscle mass compared to Europeans at the same body weight. Since BMI doesn't distinguish between fat and muscle, a lower BMI threshold is needed to capture the same level of metabolic risk in Indians. This also means Indian men and women who exercise and build muscle may have a slightly elevated BMI without it being a concern — context always matters.

Limitations of BMI — What BMI Doesn't Tell You

BMI is a useful starting point but a poor individual diagnostic tool. Here's what it misses:

1. Fat vs. Muscle

A muscular athlete can have a BMI of 27 (classified as overweight) while being extremely healthy. Conversely, someone with a "normal" BMI of 22 can have 30%+ body fat and multiple metabolic risk factors. Always pair BMI with waist circumference and lifestyle assessment.

2. Fat Distribution

Where you carry fat matters enormously. Abdominal fat (apple shape) is far more dangerous than hip-and-thigh fat (pear shape). Waist-to-height ratio is actually a better predictor of cardiovascular risk than BMI. A rough guideline: your waist circumference should be less than half your height.

3. Age and Sex

Women naturally have more body fat than men at the same BMI. Older adults who lose muscle mass can have a "normal" BMI while being metabolically unhealthy. Younger adults can tolerate slightly higher BMI if it's due to muscle.

4. Ethnicity

BMI was developed using European populations. Beyond South Asians, different populations have different body composition norms. The cutoffs recommended for Indians do not apply directly to sub-Saharan Africans or Pacific Islanders, for example.

Use BMI as a starting point. For a more complete picture of your health, combine BMI with: waist circumference, fasting blood glucose, blood pressure, and HbA1c (for diabetes risk). These together give a much clearer health snapshot than BMI alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good BMI for an Indian woman? +

For Indian women, a BMI between 18.5 and 22.9 is considered healthy by Asian standards. A BMI of 23 or above indicates overweight, and 25 or above indicates obesity. These thresholds are lower than Western standards because South Asian women tend to develop metabolic diseases at lower BMI levels.

Is BMI 23 overweight for Indians? +

Yes, by Asian/Indian standards, a BMI of 23 is classified as overweight. While a BMI of 23 is considered perfectly healthy in Western charts, multiple studies in India and South Asia have found increased metabolic risk (especially for diabetes and heart disease) starting from BMI 23.

My BMI says I'm overweight but I feel healthy. What should I do? +

Don't panic — BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. If you're physically active, eat well, and have normal blood sugar, cholesterol, and blood pressure, a slightly elevated BMI may not be medically significant. Measure your waist circumference and see a doctor for metabolic blood tests to get a complete picture.

Can I be healthy at BMI 25 or above? +

For Indians, BMI 25+ is in the obese range and does carry increased health risk on a population level. However, individual health outcomes depend on many factors — fitness level, metabolic health markers, lifestyle habits, and family history. Some people at BMI 25–27 who are active and have clean metabolic bloodwork may not need urgent intervention; others at a lower BMI with poor metabolic markers might. See a doctor for personalised advice.

Is BMI accurate during pregnancy? +

No. BMI is not accurate or relevant during pregnancy. Weight gain during pregnancy is normal and necessary. Pre-pregnancy BMI is used to guide appropriate weight gain during pregnancy, but this should be managed by your OB-GYN, not a calculator.
