Surya Namaskar for Weight Loss: How Many Calories Does It Actually Burn?

The calorie math behind Surya Namaskar — at different speeds, body weights, and fitness levels — supported by sports science research.

👤Kavitha Nair
📅11 June 2025
🔄Updated 25 June 2025
⏱ 5 min read

📌 Key Takeaways

  • One round of Surya Namaskar burns approximately 13–15 kcal for a 70kg person at a moderate pace
  • 12 rounds per day (the traditional recommendation) burns roughly 150–180 kcal for most adults
  • At a fast pace (cardio yoga), 24 rounds can burn 300–350 kcal — comparable to a 30-minute jog
  • Surya Namaskar also improves flexibility, posture, and mental focus beyond just calorie burning
  • Combining Surya Namaskar with a calorie-controlled Indian diet produces sustainable fat loss

The Science of Surya Namaskar and Calorie Burn

Surya Namaskar (Sun Salutation) is a sequence of 12 yoga postures performed in a continuous flow. Unlike static yoga poses, the dynamic movement between postures elevates heart rate, engages multiple muscle groups simultaneously, and creates both aerobic and anaerobic demand depending on the speed of practice.

📊

A 2011 study published in the Indian Journal of Physiology and Pharmacology found that 24 rounds of Surya Namaskar at a moderate pace (one round every 96 seconds) produced a mean heart rate of 123 bpm — equivalent to moderate-intensity aerobic exercise.

The calorie burn varies significantly based on three factors: body weight, speed of practice, and fitness level. Heavier individuals burn more calories per round; faster practice elevates heart rate more; and less fit individuals have a higher relative effort per round.

For 12 rounds (the traditional daily practice): A 70kg person burns approximately 150–175 kcal at a moderate pace, and up to 230 kcal at a fast pace. This takes 18–20 minutes at moderate speed.

💡

Surya Namaskar burns calories comparable to jogging but with far lower impact on joints — making it particularly suitable for older adults, those with knee issues, or people who are overweight and cannot yet sustain high-impact cardio.
