Intermittent Fasting for Indians: Does It Work with Indian Meal Timings?

Late dinners, festival feasting, office chai breaks — we adapt the 16:8 and 5:2 fasting protocols to real Indian routines without disrupting family life.

👤Dr. Ananya Rao
📅15 June 2025
🔄Updated 29 June 2025
⏱ 7 min read

📌 Key Takeaways

  • Intermittent fasting (IF) works by creating a calorie deficit — not through any metabolic magic
  • The 12:12 protocol (12 hours fasting) is the most realistic starting point for most Indians
  • Late Indian dinners make the 16:8 protocol challenging — timing the window 1–9 PM is one solution
  • Chai with milk breaks the fast — switch to black tea or plain water during the fasting window
  • IF is not suitable for diabetics on medication, pregnant women, or those with a history of eating disorders

What Is Intermittent Fasting and How Does It Work?

Intermittent fasting (IF) is an eating pattern that cycles between periods of fasting and eating. Unlike traditional diets that restrict what you eat, IF restricts when you eat. The most popular protocols are:

A 2020 meta-analysis in Obesity Reviews found that intermittent fasting produced weight loss of 0.8–13% of body weight over 8–24 weeks — comparable to continuous calorie restriction, with no significant metabolic advantage beyond the calorie deficit created.

The Indian Meal Timing Problem

Standard IF advice assumes a Western meal pattern: breakfast at 7–8 AM, lunch at noon, dinner at 6–7 PM. The Indian meal pattern is very different:

  • Chai at 6–7 AM: With milk and sugar, this breaks the fast immediately — a problem for strict IF.
  • Late dinners: Most urban Indian families eat dinner at 9–10 PM, making a 7 PM cutoff socially difficult.
  • Festival and social eating: Weddings, festivals, and family functions are non-negotiable calorie events.
  • Office chai culture: Multiple chai breaks throughout the workday make fasting windows difficult to maintain.
Adaptation for India — The 1 PM to 9 PM eating window: This eating window accommodates a late lunch (1–2 PM) and a late dinner (8–9 PM) while maintaining the 16-hour fasting window overnight. Black tea or coffee replaces the morning chai.

IF Schedules Adapted for Indian Lifestyles

☀️Office Worker (16:8)
  • 6 AM: Black tea or water
  • 1 PM: First meal — dal rice or roti + sabzi
  • 4 PM: Snack — curd or fruit
  • 8:30 PM: Last meal — light dinner
  • Fast resumes 9 PM to 1 PM next day
Works well for desk job schedules
🏠Homemaker (12:12)
  • 7 AM: Chai with minimal milk (or skip)
  • 8 AM: Breakfast — idli or chilla
  • 1 PM: Lunch — full thali
  • 5 PM: Light snack — fruit or curd
  • 7:30 PM: Early light dinner
Best starting protocol for most people
💼5:2 Protocol
  • Mon/Thu: 500 kcal days (khichdi + salad)
  • Other days: Normal Indian eating
  • No timing restriction on normal days
  • Suited to those with social commitments
Best for those who cannot skip morning chai
🌙Festival Adaptation
  • Shift eating window on festival days
  • Return to protocol the following day
  • Do not compensate by restricting more
  • Focus on portion control during the feast
Flexibility is key to long-term adherence

Who Should Not Do Intermittent Fasting

Intermittent fasting is not appropriate for everyone. Avoid IF if you:

  • Have Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes and are on insulin or sulfonylurea medications (risk of hypoglycemia)
  • Are pregnant or breastfeeding
  • Have a history of eating disorders (anorexia, bulimia)
  • Are underweight (BMI below 18.5)
  • Are under 18 years of age
  • Have a history of hypoglycemia or low blood pressure
⚠️

Always consult your physician before starting any fasting protocol if you have an existing medical condition or are on regular medication. The liver and kidneys require consistent hydration — drink at least 2–3 litres of plain water during the fasting window.
