Protein for Vegetarian Indians: Getting 100g a Day Without Supplements

Dal, paneer, soy, sattu, moong — a complete guide to hitting your daily protein target using only Indian vegetarian food sources.

👤Rohan Mehta
📅19 June 2025
🔄Updated 2 July 2025
⏱ 6 min read

📌 Key Takeaways

  • 100g of protein per day is achievable on a vegetarian Indian diet without any supplements
  • Soy chunks (nutrela) are the most protein-dense vegetarian food available in India at 52g/100g dry weight
  • Combining complementary proteins (dal + rice, roti + curd) creates complete amino acid profiles
  • Spreading protein across 4–5 meals is more effective than consuming it all at once
  • Sattu is an underrated protein source — 30g provides 10g of protein at a fraction of supplement costs

The Indian Vegetarian Protein Gap

India has one of the highest rates of protein deficiency in the world. The National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5) found that a significant proportion of Indian adults consume less than 0.6g of protein per kg of body weight — well below the ICMR recommendation of 0.8–1g/kg, and far below the 1.2–1.6g/kg recommended for active individuals.

📊

For a 70 kg active individual, getting 100g of protein per day from vegetarian Indian sources requires deliberate planning — but it is entirely achievable without expensive supplements, protein powders, or imported foods.

The key is understanding which Indian foods are protein-dense and building meals around them — rather than treating protein as an afterthought.

A Sample 100g Protein Day (Vegetarian)

🌅Breakfast — 28g protein
  • 2 moong dal chillas: 14g
  • 200g low-fat curd: 11g
  • 1 tbsp peanuts: 3g
~420 kcal
🍱Lunch — 30g protein
  • 100g low-fat paneer curry: 18g
  • 1 katori toor dal: 8g
  • 2 rotis: 4g
~520 kcal
☕Snack — 20g protein
  • Sattu drink (30g sattu): 10g
  • 30g soy chunks (roasted): 14g
~220 kcal
🌙Dinner — 22g protein
  • 1 katori rajma: 9g
  • 1 katori chana dal: 9g
  • 100g sprouted salad: 4g
~420 kcal

Total: ~100g protein · ~1,580 kcal. Adjust quantities to match your TDEE.

Practical Tips for Hitting Protein Targets

1

Add soy chunks to everything. Boiled and squeezed soy chunks take on any flavour — add to biryani, pulao, sabzis, and sandwiches for 14g of protein per 30g serving.

2

Switch from full-fat to low-fat paneer. Low-fat paneer has 18g of protein per 100g vs. 14g in full-fat — and 95 fewer calories. Make it at home with low-fat milk for maximum savings.

3

Drink sattu morning and evening. 2 tbsp sattu in a glass of water with lemon and salt is a 10-minute preparation that delivers 10g of protein for ₹5.

4

Combine dals strategically. No single dal contains all essential amino acids. Mixing toor + moong, or rajma + rice, creates complementary protein profiles with complete amino acid coverage.
