Why Your BMI Doesn't Tell the Whole Story for Indian Bodies

South Asians carry metabolic risk at lower BMI values than Western populations. Here is what to measure instead — and what Indian health guidelines actually say.

👤Dr. Ananya Rao
📅7 June 2025
🔄Updated 22 June 2025
⏱ 6 min read

📌 Key Takeaways

  • South Asians develop metabolic risk (diabetes, heart disease) at lower BMI values than Western populations
  • The WHO recommends a lower BMI cutoff of 23 for overweight in South Asian adults
  • Waist circumference is a better predictor of metabolic risk than BMI for Indians
  • Body fat percentage and visceral fat are the metrics that actually matter
  • A 'normal' BMI does not rule out metabolic syndrome in Indian adults

What BMI Actually Measures (And What It Does Not)

Body Mass Index (BMI) is calculated by dividing your weight in kilograms by the square of your height in metres. It was developed by Belgian statistician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s — not as a clinical health tool, but as a population-level statistical measure. It was never designed to assess individual health risk.

📊

BMI cannot distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass. A professional cricketer with high muscle mass may have a BMI of 27 (classified as overweight), while a sedentary person with high visceral fat but low muscle may have a BMI of 22 (classified as normal).

Despite these limitations, BMI remains widely used because it requires only two easily measurable inputs. The problem arises when standard BMI cutoffs developed from European populations are applied to South Asians, who have very different body composition profiles.

The South Asian BMI Problem

South Asians — including Indians, Pakistanis, Bangladeshis, and Sri Lankans — have significantly higher body fat percentages at the same BMI compared to Europeans. At a BMI of 25 (the Western cutoff for overweight), South Asians already have the metabolic risk profile of a European person at BMI 30 (obese).

ClassificationStandard WHO CutoffRevised South Asian Cutoff
UnderweightBelow 18.5Below 18.5
Normal weight18.5 – 24.918.5 – 22.9
Overweight25.0 – 29.923.0 – 27.4
Obese30.0 and above27.5 and above
ICMR & WHO joint guidance (2004): The appropriate BMI cutoff for overweight in South Asian adults is 23 kg/m², not 25 kg/m². This means millions of Indians classified as "normal" by Western standards are actually at elevated metabolic risk.

Better Metrics to Track for Indians

These four measurements give a more complete picture of metabolic health than BMI alone:

1

Waist circumference: Risk threshold for Indians is above 90cm (men) and 80cm (women) — lower than Western guidelines of 102cm and 88cm.

2

Waist-to-height ratio: Divide your waist measurement by your height. A ratio above 0.5 indicates central obesity and elevated cardiovascular risk.

3

Fasting blood glucose: Indians are 4× more likely to develop Type 2 diabetes than Europeans at the same weight. Test annually if BMI exceeds 23.

4

HbA1c: The 3-month blood sugar average. A reading above 5.7% indicates pre-diabetes — common in Indians at weights considered "normal" by Western standards.

"An Indian with a BMI of 24 has the cardiovascular risk of a European with a BMI of 28. We need to stop using Western thresholds to assess Indian bodies."

— Dr. V. Mohan, Madras Diabetes Research Foundation

What to Do If Your BMI Is in the 'Normal' Range

If your BMI falls between 18.5 and 24.9, do not assume you are metabolically healthy. Take these steps:

  1. Measure your waist circumference at the navel level. If it exceeds 90cm (men) or 80cm (women), you have central obesity regardless of your BMI.
  2. Get a fasting blood glucose test and an HbA1c. These are the earliest indicators of insulin resistance and pre-diabetes.
  3. Ask your doctor for a lipid profile. High triglycerides and low HDL are common in Indians with normal BMI and indicate metabolic syndrome.
  4. Track body fat percentage, not just weight. A DEXA scan is the gold standard, but bioelectrical impedance scales (widely available in Indian gyms and clinics) provide a reasonable estimate.
🏥

The National Programme for Prevention and Control of Cancer, Diabetes, CVD, and Stroke (NPCDCS) recommends annual metabolic screening for all Indians above the age of 30, irrespective of BMI.
